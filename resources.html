Shade, L. R. (2019, January 13). Anita Borg. Retrieved January 28, 2019, from https://www.britannica.com/biography/Anita-Borg

Anita Borg is one of the major figure heads when it comes down to women in STEM, especially in computing and technology. Borg was extremely dissatisfied with the lack of women in the computing, and she took many initiatives to increase women's participation in the field, becoming an advocate for women in computing; she felt that the lack of women also made issues of equity arise in the world of computing, which is predominantly male field. She started the Systers organization which sole purpose was to be an electronic community for women in computing, and eventually the organization grew exponentially in more than 50 countries. Borg, then, co-founded the Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing in honor of Grace Hopper (a famous woman that helped pioneer early technology), and it is a conference where women in computing all over the world come and celebrate the contributions and highlights of women in computing. She also created an institution for women in technology, later renamed after her in her honor. One of her major contributions was when she led a research group because rather than focusing on how computers worked, she was interested in how they can be used to link people.

Women Who Inspire Us. (n.d.). Retrieved February 16, 2019, from http://www.girlgeeks.org/innergeek/inspiringwomen/borg.shtml

Anita Borg's legacy has inspired many women in the world of computing. Borg is a former member of the staff at Xerox's Palo Alto Research Center as she jump-started her career, and she created the Institute for Women and Technology to help increase recognition and impact that women have in the world of technology because women were not treated as equals in the world of technology. She spent majority of her early career assisting in the development of operational systems for Digital Equipment Corporation, Nixdorf Computer, and Auragen Systems Corporation, and even created Mecca, an email system for her Systers organization to help connect women in technology across the world in a virtual community. She is an advocate for many issues of women in computing which include the Association for Computing Machinery, the Computing Research Association and the National Academy of Engineering, along with being one of the co-founders for the Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing.

Oral History: Anita Borg [Interview by J. Abbate]. (2001, January 5). Retrieved February 16, 2019, from https://ethw.org/Oral-History:Anita_Borg

Anita Borg studied at NYU and eventually went on to pursue a Ph.D.. She had a mentality to "...never have to depend on someone for support again..." Borg's thesis had theoretically dealt with operating systems and the synchronization of machines and how they worked. This lead to her being a member of her first computing occupation at Auragen Systems Corporation, and Borg named the developing operating system TARGON, which ended up being a product of Nixdorf in Germany. Borg made her official debut at a conference directed by Association for Computing Machinery (ACM), this led to Borg becoming one of the most influential people of computing. This milestone assisted her in obtaining a job with Digital Equipment Corporation, and she then relocated from Germany to California. At this point, she began the development of Mecca, which initially allowed Borg and other women in computing communicate, being one of the original ways to electronically mail letters, etc, before the world wide web was created.

Anita Borg's personal associates and friends remember her in this documentary. She was very adamant about changing the world through her advocacy for women because she noticed the lack of women in the world of technology. She started Systers in 1987, and this allowed many women to connect and how they can utilize technology to bring awareness to women's contributions to technology. It initially started just to bring awareness to women in operating systems, but eventually grew into all disciplines of computer science. Systers is one of the earlier representations of communicating online before online communities were created. She met many of her associates through Systers, and Systers led to the development of the Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing, an event hosted every year to celebrate women in the world of computers. Anita Borg then created the Institute for Women In Technology.

She received her Ph.D. of computer science at New York University in 1981.
